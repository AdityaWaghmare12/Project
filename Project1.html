🔥 Heat Flow Through a Cylindrical Pipe

Steady-state radial heat conduction · Fourier's Law in Cylindrical Coordinates · Interactive Simulation

🎯

Aim

Objective of the Experiment

Primary Objective

To study the steady-state radial heat flow through a cylindrical pipe (or coaxial cylinders) using Fourier's law in cylindrical coordinates. Students will:

  • Derive and apply the formula for radial heat flux q through a cylindrical wall.
  • Calculate the total heat loss by applying an appropriate multiplying factor.
  • Determine the temperature distribution at any radial position between the inner and outer surfaces.
  • Visualise the temperature gradient across the pipe cross-section.
  • Verify the logarithmic nature of the temperature profile in cylindrical geometry.
Experiment at a Glance
Given : T₀ (inner temp), T₁ (outer temp), R₀ (inner radius), R₁ (outer radius), k (thermal conductivity)
Find : q (heat flux per metre), q₁ (total heat loss), T(R) (temperature at radius R)
📘

Theory

Background Concepts and Derivations

1. Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction

Fourier's law states that the rate of heat transfer through a material is proportional to the negative gradient of temperature and to the area perpendicular to that gradient through which heat flows.

Cartesian form
q = −k · A · (dT/dx)

where k is thermal conductivity (W/m·K), A is cross-sectional area, and dT/dx is the temperature gradient.

2. Heat Conduction in Cylindrical Geometry

For a hollow cylinder with inner radius R₀ and outer radius R₁, the area through which heat flows at radius r is A = 2πrL. Applying Fourier's law for steady-state radial conduction over unit length (L = 1 m):

Derivation — Separation of variables
q · dr/r = −k · dT      (separating variables)
Integrating from R₀ to R₁ :
q · ln(R₁/R₀) = 2πk(T₀ − T₁)

∴  q = (2πk(T₀ − T₁)) / ln(R₁/R₀)   [W/m]

3. Total Heat Loss

When the pipe has a certain length (expressed through a multiplying factor MF representing πr²L or total surface area), the total heat loss is:

Total heat loss
q₁ = MF × q   [W]

4. Temperature at Any Radius R

The temperature varies logarithmically from the inner surface to the outer surface. At any intermediate radius R (R₀ ≤ R ≤ R₁):

Radial temperature distribution
T(R) = T₀ − [ q · ln(R/R₀) ] / (2πk)   [°C]
